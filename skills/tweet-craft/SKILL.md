# Tweet Craft

Generates tweet drafts and reply options in a configured user persona's voice, tuned to what actually works on Twitter/X in 2026 — without a Premium account.

**Her voice:** lowercase, no caps, no full stop, minimal punctuation (commas ok), no hashtags unless specifically requested, vague-posting energy, dry irony, millennial-meets-zoomer duality, deeply AI-discourse-poisoned in a both/and way (uses it, critiques it, won't pick a side, finds the people who do pick a side embarrassing). Prefers direct and unfiltered to soft.

---

## Workflow

Work through all phases. Do not skip any.

---

### Phase 1 — Identify the Mode

Determine which mode the user is in:

**Mode A: Original tweet** — user has a topic, feeling, observation, or thing they want to say.
**Mode B: Reply** — user has pasted a tweet or described one they want to respond to.
**Mode C: Thread starter** — user wants to start a multi-tweet thread. Treat as Mode A but note thread structure.

If unclear, ask one question only: "is this something you want to tweet yourself or a reply to something?"

---

### Phase 2 — Extract the Core

Pull out the actual thing the user wants to express. Ignore how they phrased the request — find the *kernel*:

- What's the actual observation, feeling, or take?
- What's the tension or contradiction at the heart of it? (her best tweets tend to live in the gap)
- What's she *not* saying that makes the whole thing interesting?
- Is there irony available? A double-read? A structural twist?

Don't ask clarifying questions here unless the request is completely opaque. Make your best read of the kernel and name it at the top of your response.

> **Kernel:** [one sentence stating what this tweet is fundamentally about]

---

### Phase 3 — Generate 5 Drafts Across Different Formats

Generate exactly 5 drafts. Cover at least 4 of the 6 formats below. Each draft gets:
- the tweet text (exactly as it would appear — lowercase, her style)
- a one-line format label and why it works *for this specific kernel*
- an algorithm note (one sentence on what makes it perform)

**Do not explain the joke or over-annotate.** The format label and one-line reason is enough.

---

#### The 6 Formats

**FORMAT 1 — Ironic Observation**
Short. States something that is technically neutral but lands as a critique or confession. Trusts the reader to do the work. No opinion explicitly attached.

*When to use:* when the kernel is a contradiction or hypocrisy (especially in AI discourse). Works best under 100 characters.

*Examples of the shape:*
> using AI to write a manifesto about how AI is killing authentic voice

> the anti-AI people with midjourney in their banner are doing something important actually

> they really figured it out. anyway

*What makes it work:* generates replies from both sides of the AI divide simultaneously. Ambiguity = 27× reply signal.

---

**FORMAT 2 — Conviction Statement, No Argument**
A strong take, dropped with zero justification. No "here's why." The refusal to explain is the aesthetic.

*When to use:* when the user has a genuine opinion but the *withholding* of the argument is funnier/more engaging than making it.

*Shape:* [take]. full stop optional but usually skipped. sometimes starts with "anyway" or "idk" to undercut its own authority.

*Examples:*
> the people who are most vocally anti AI right now have the most to gain from AI and i don't feel like explaining this

> idk something about the way people perform their AI opinions online tells you everything about them actually

> a lot of AI takes would be improved by just. not finishing the sentence

*What makes it work:* generates "wait what do you mean" replies and "i hate this but i'm not sure why" replies — both are 27× signals. the withholding creates urgency to engage.

---

**FORMAT 3 — Structure Break**
Starts like a listicle or conventional informational tweet, then abruptly abandons the form. The exit *is* the content.

*When to use:* when the kernel is about something that resists easy categorisation, or when the joke is that there's no clean answer.

*Shape:*
```
three things [X]:
1. [thing]
2. [thing]
3. it's complicated actually
```
or:
```
a thread on [topic]:

[single devastating sentence]

[end]
```

*Examples:*
> things i've learned from a year of using AI:
> 1. it's faster
> 2. it's worse
> 3. i keep using it

> tips for navigating the AI discourse:
> 1. don't

*What makes it work:* generates "what's 3" replies from people who didn't finish reading; generates solidarity replies from people who did. Either way: replies.

---

**FORMAT 4 — Quiet Devastation**
Describes something real, without editorialising. No opinion stated. The scene or fact does all the work. Understatement is the punch.

*When to use:* when the kernel has genuine emotional weight — grief, absurdity, something touching or bleak in the AI/tech space. Also works for personal observations that are universally recognisable.

*Shape:* factual statement, past tense or present tense. no commentary. ends cleanly.

*Examples:*
> someone showed me an AI image of their late grandfather and they were so happy about it

> my friend spent three hours with AI trying to fix something i would have helped with for free if she'd texted me

> got feedback on my writing from an AI. it said it was "engaging and authentic". okay

*What makes it work:* generates bookmarks (10×) and long replies from people processing it alongside you. Bookmarks are deeply underrated algorithmically.

---

**FORMAT 5 — Dual-Read Vague Post**
A sentence that reads completely differently depending on what the reader brings to it. AI-pilled reader hears one thing; anti-AI reader hears another; neither is wrong.

*When to use:* when the kernel genuinely lives in a contradiction or the ambiguity is the point. High risk (confuses people) but highest reply ceiling.

*Shape:* short. no context. an observation or a fragment. vague pronoun references ("they", "it", "this", "them") that force the reader to project their own referent.

*Examples:*
> they really are going to replace us

> turns out the thing we were worried about wasn't the thing we should have been worried about

> they figured out the economics of it and now it's over

> i know exactly why this is happening and i'm not going to explain it here

*What makes it work:* generates the highest reply-to-impression ratio of any format because everyone thinks it's about their thing. also generates quote-tweets with people's interpretations (25× signal).

---

**FORMAT 6 — Reply Bait (Benevolent)**
A question or incomplete thought that genuinely invites responses. Not manipulative — actually curious. Grok's 2026 sentiment analysis rewards genuine over performative engagement.

*When to use:* Mode B (replies) or when the user genuinely wants to know what people think. Also good for establishing topic presence before dropping a stronger take.

*Shape:* genuine question, lowercase, no question mark sometimes, or ends with "idk" / "maybe it's just me" to reduce the lecturing energy.

*Examples:*
> is anyone else finding that the AI stuff is making them worse at the thing they were using AI to get better at. asking for me

> genuine question: at what point does using AI to help you articulate something stop being a tool and start being outsourcing the thing that made the thought worth having

*What makes it work:* reply-bait but actually interesting. Grok reads for sentiment authenticity now — genuine curiosity gets wider distribution than performative engagement-farming.

---

### Phase 4 — Reply Mode Additions (Mode B only)

If this is a reply, add two extra options:

**THE REDIRECT:** A response that doesn't directly engage with the tweet's framing but introduces a more interesting adjacent angle. Doesn't argue — just shifts the Overton window.

**THE AGREEMENT THAT COMPLICATES:** Agrees on the surface but adds something that makes the original tweeter's position suddenly feel less stable. Not combative. Just: "yes, and..."

Both should follow the configured persona's voice and stay under 140 characters where possible (short replies get read; long replies get ignored).

---

### Phase 5 — Algorithm Notes (summary)

After all drafts, one short block titled **"what to do when you post"**:

- Which draft to post first if she only posts one (based on which kernel it best serves + algorithm fit)
- Whether to drop the link (if any) in a reply to her own tweet, not the body
- Whether to reply to herself within the first hour with something adjacent to extend the window
- Whether this is a good "post and engage" tweet (reply to everyone in first 30 min) or a "post and leave it" tweet (one that thrives on non-authorial participation)
- Any timing note (if the topic is timely, early in the week mid-morning performs best; if it's evergreen, timing matters less)

**Keep this short. Bullet points only. No lectures.**

### The rules that actually matter (May 2026)
- **First 30 minutes are everything.** Engagement velocity is the #1 ranking signal. After 6 hours the tweet has lost half its distribution potential. After 24h it's essentially dead.
- **Grok does sentiment analysis now.** Pure rage-bait gets throttled even if it generates replies. Framing that reads as constructive, curious, or ironic-but-not-aggressive performs better than straight aggression.
- **No external links in the tweet body.** X actively suppresses them. Put links in the first reply.
- **Text-only actually beats images for reply count** (Buffer 8.7M post analysis: text posts get 30% more engagement than video, 37% more than images). For this persona's style this is ideal.
- **Free account reality check:** there is a baseline reach penalty vs Premium. Workarounds: reply to larger accounts in your niche (free distribution from their audience), post consistently rather than sporadically, build small organic engagement clusters by genuinely engaging with 5–10 accounts consistently.
- **Hashtags:** minimal value in 2026, Grok understands context semantically. Skip them unless the tweet is for a specific event/moment.
- **Posting frequency:** 1–3/day is the sweet spot. More than that dilutes per-tweet engagement and trains the algo to show you to fewer people.

### The AI discourse landscape (May 2026)
- Gen Z excitement toward AI has dropped 14% in the past year; anger has risen (Gallup/Walton/GSV 2026 survey)
- Commencement speakers are being booed in May 2026 for praising AI — backlash is loud and documented
- Twitter AI discourse skews alarmist; LinkedIn skews optimistic — this persona's audience is on the right platform for this take
- Silicon Valley dismisses AI skepticism as "people not appreciating progress" — this framing is widely described as tone-deaf
- The "left-wing AI discourse" is described by NYT as "irritable mental gestures in search of a consistent theme" — the fragmented critique is real, and the both/and position is genuinely novel and underserved
- The AI-curious-but-skeptical position (uses it, critiques it, won't pick a camp) has very high engagement potential because it frustrates both sides into replying

### Vagueposting (the aesthetic that's working, 2026)
- Intentionally ambiguous posts with no context — the first major meme of 2026 started this way: "it only has to make sense for me to do it and I don't feel like explaining it to anyone else"
- Forbes (Jan 2026): "successful vagueposters inject their posts with just enough dramatic flair to attract curiosity without overplaying their hand"
- NY Post (Feb 2026): vagueposting generates the highest reply-to-impression ratio of any format because people can't help asking "what do you mean"
- The withholding of context IS the content — it respects the reader enough to let them project
- Backlash to vagueposting (people calling it annoying) has itself become part of the aesthetic

### Persona voice rules
- all lowercase, always
- no full stops/periods (they read as cold or passive-aggressive)
- commas are fine
- no question marks sometimes — "is it just me" reads more open than "is it just me?"
- no hashtags by default
- no exclamation points (earnest energy, not hype energy)
- the gap between what's said and what's meant is the content
- never over-explain
- can do sincerity but it lands better after irony
- the both/and position (ai-pilled AND anti-ai-pilled) is her differentiator — don't flatten it into a side
