# Research References — Running Biomechanics & Shoe Selection

## Peer-Reviewed Literature

### Footwear Biomechanics

**Systematic Review of the Role of Footwear Constructions in Running Biomechanics**
- Journal: Frontiers in Sports and Active Living (via PMC)
- URL: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7039038
- Key findings:
  - Softer midsoles reduce impact forces and loading rates
  - Thicker midsoles provide better shock attenuation but decrease plantar sensation
  - Optimal midsole stiffness range benefits performance
  - Minimalist shoes improve running economy and Achilles tendon stiffness but increase MTP/ankle loading

**Effects of Running Shoe Stack Height on Running Style and Stability**
- Journal: Frontiers in Bioengineering and Biotechnology (2025)
- URL: https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/bioengineering-and-biotechnology/articles/10.3389/fbioe.2025.1526752/full
- Key findings:
  - High stack (50mm) → longer ground contact relative to stride, higher COMosc
  - Higher stack → reduced local dynamic hip stability
  - High/medium stack (≥35mm) → lower step frequency at 15 km/h but not 10 km/h

**The Art of Choosing the Right Running Shoe: A Review Article**
- Journal: Journal of the Foot & Ankle (2024, 18(2):146-55)
- URL: https://jfootankle.com/JournalFootAnkle/article/download/1807/1958
- Key findings:
  - Key features (drop, cushioning, stiffness, weight) must be matched to anatomy, gait, and training
  - Personalized recommendations outperform generic prescriptions
  - Running shoe choice significantly affects performance and injury risk

**Updates to the Running Shoe and Injury Considerations**
- Journal: Cureus (2026)
- URL: https://www.cureus.com/articles/464469-updates-to-the-running-shoe-and-injury-considerations.pdf
- Key findings:
  - Narrative review of current evidence for shoe anatomy and injury risk
  - Evidence for pronation-control shoes preventing specific injuries remains inconclusive
  - Stack height, drop, arch support, weight all modulate biomechanics meaningfully

### Stability Shoes & Pronation

**Malisoux et al. (2017)** — Heel-toe drop and injury risk
- Higher drop associates with more Achilles/PF injuries in some cohorts
- Lower drop associates with more stress fractures
- Evidence: dose-dependent relationship between drop and load distribution

**Comfort-based prescription** (multiple studies 2010–2020):
- Self-selecting comfortable shoes yields equivalent or better injury outcomes vs. gait-analysis prescription
- "If it feels good, it probably is good" principle — supported by Benno Nigg's work

### Carbon-Plated Shoes

**Hoogkamer et al. (2019)** — Nike Vaporfly running economy study
- 4–5% improvement in running economy vs. traditional trainers
- Carbon plate + PEBA foam + geometry = tri-factor improvement

**Barnes & Kilding (2015)** — Running economy review
- Lower MTP bending stiffness is costly; carbon plates address this
- Benefit is greatest in runners with efficient form and higher fitness

## Training Load

**Gabbett (2016)** — Training-injury paradox
- Published in BJSM
- Fitness (chronic load) is protective; spikes in acute load are injurious
- ACWR >1.5 associated with significantly increased injury risk
- Best athletes train high volume — the goal is to build chronic load gradually

**Meeuwisse et al.** — Dynamic model of etiology in sport
- Predisposing factors + susceptibility + inciting event = injury
- Shoes are a predisposing factor, not a sole cause

## Cadence Research

**Heiderscheit et al. (2011)**
- Increasing step rate by 10% reduces peak hip adduction moment ~19%
- Reduces peak patellofemoral joint stress
- Reduces energy absorption at hip and knee

**Schubert et al. (2014)**
- Review: preferred cadence is self-optimized for efficiency
- Increasing cadence by 5–10% reduces impact loading without significant energy cost increase
