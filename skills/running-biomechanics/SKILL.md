# Running Biomechanics & Shoe Selection Skill

Use this skill for any question about how running shoes interact with a runner's biomechanics,
how to match shoes to gait or training context, how to reason about injury risk from footwear
or training load, or how to advise on form and technique.

---

## 1. Gait Cycle & Foot Strike

### The Running Gait Cycle
```
Stance phase (contact)           |  Float phase (airborne)
─────────────────────────────────|──────────────────────────
Initial contact → Loading → Midstance → Terminal stance → Pre-swing → Swing
```

**Ground contact time** at easy pace ~200–250ms; at race pace ~150–180ms.
**Stance phase** = ~40% of gait cycle at easy pace; less at speed.

### Foot Strike Patterns

| Pattern | Contact point | Typical association |
|---|---|---|
| **Rearfoot / heel strike** | Lateral heel | Common (~75% of rec. runners), high braking impulse |
| **Midfoot strike** | Ball of foot + heel simultaneously | Efficient energy transfer, lower braking forces |
| **Forefoot strike** | Ball of foot only | Sprinters; increased Achilles/calf load, lower knee load |

**Key insight:** Foot strike pattern is not immutably determined by shoe type, but shoes influence it:
- High drop (10–12mm) facilitates heel strike
- Low drop (0–5mm) encourages more midfoot loading
- Changing strike pattern without adaptation → Achilles/calf overload

**Do not prescribe foot strike changes without gradual transition.** The research on strike pattern
and injury rate is equivocal — what matters more is **cadence, overstriding, and load management**.

## 2. Pronation, Supination & the Stability Spectrum

### Definitions
- **Pronation:** Inward rolling of the foot after heel contact. **Normal and necessary** for shock absorption.
- **Overpronation:** Excessive inward roll — foot collapses medially past the midline.
- **Supination (underpronation):** Insufficient inward roll — foot stays on lateral edge throughout stance.

### Functional Assessment (Dynamic > Static)
| Test | Method | Finding |
|---|---|---|
| Navicular drop | Mark navicular height seated vs. standing | >10mm drop suggests excessive pronation |
| Single-leg squat | Observe knee tracking | Knee valgus = pronation tendency |
| Gait video (rear view) | 30fps+ slow motion | Assess calcaneal eversion, medial column collapse |
| Wet footprint | Static arch impression | Outdated — arch height ≠ dynamic pronation |

### Stability Shoe Categories

| Category | Mechanism | Indication |
|---|---|---|
| **Neutral** | No medial correction | Neutral/supinator gait, mild pronation |
| **Stability** | Medial post or guidance rail | Moderate overpronation |
| **Motion control** | Rigid medial post + wider base | Severe overpronation, very flat feet |

**Important caveat:** Systematic reviews show inconclusive evidence that stability shoes
*prevent* injuries vs. neutral shoes. The comfort-based prescription ("wear what feels right")
shows comparable outcomes. Use stability as a tool for comfort and support, not as an
injury-prevention guarantee.

#### Brand Implementations of Stability

| Brand | System | Character |
|---|---|---|
| New Balance | Medial post (ABZORB SBS) | Firm medial correction |
| Brooks | GuideRails | Guides knee, not just foot — less intrusive |
| ASICS | LITETRUSS | Lightweight medial support, less aggressive |
| HOKA | J-Frame (Gaviota) | Firmer medial foam, low drop |
| Saucony | Medial post (PWRRUN firm) | Standard density differential |
| Mizuno | Fan Wave plate | Structural wave, not foam post |

## 3. Heel-Toe Drop — Clinical Reasoning

**Drop = heel stack height − forefoot stack height**

### Drop and Load Distribution

| Drop | Characteristic | Load shift |
|---|---|---|
| **0mm (zero)** | Equal heel/forefoot height | Maximum Achilles/calf load; minimum knee load |
| **4–6mm (low)** | Slight heel elevation | Balanced — moderate Achilles, moderate knee |
| **8–10mm (moderate)** | Significant heel lift | Reduced Achilles load; some increased knee stress |
| **12mm (high)** | Traditional running shoe | Lowest Achilles/calf demand; highest knee flexion moment |

### Clinical Applications

**Prescribe LOWER drop for:**
- Runners with patellofemoral pain syndrome (runner's knee)
- Runners with IT band syndrome (ITBS)
- Runners wanting to strengthen intrinsic foot muscles and Achilles
- Barefoot/minimalist shoe advocates (gradual transition required)

**Prescribe HIGHER drop for:**
- Runners with Achilles tendinopathy or chronic calf tightness
- Plantar fasciitis (may also need arch support)
- Beginners or those with limited ankle dorsiflexion
- Transitioning from years of high-drop shoes

**Transition protocol** when changing drop significantly (>4mm difference):
1. Introduce new shoe for ≤20% of weekly mileage
2. Increase by 5–10% per week over 6–8 weeks
3. Monitor Achilles/calf soreness as limiting factor
4. Never switch abruptly from 12mm to 0mm — calf/Achilles injury risk is very high

## 4. Stack Height — Biomechanical Effects

Research (Frontiers in Bioengineering, 2025; PMC systematic review):

| Stack | Effect |
|---|---|
| **High (≥40mm)** | Increased COMosc (center of mass oscillation), reduced hip dynamic stability, longer ground contact time at speed |
| **Medium (30–39mm)** | Moderate COMosc; good balance of cushion and proprioception |
| **Low (≤29mm)** | Higher proprioception, lower COMosc, faster feedback — firmer ride |

### When High Stack Matters

**Benefits:**
- Hard surfaces (concrete, asphalt) — attenuates high repetitive impact
- Ultra/long-distance running — reduces fatigue from cumulative loading
- Walkers — reduced joint stress over extended time on feet
- Post-injury return-to-run — reduced acute loading

**Risks/tradeoffs:**
- Reduced proprioception → ankle instability risk (especially on uneven ground)
- Higher COMosc → less efficient at speed
- Reduced hip stability → compensatory gait patterns over time
- Sensation of instability ("being on stilts") in narrow models

**Practical guidance:** Rotate high-stack shoes with lower-stack alternatives.
Do not run all miles in max-cushion shoes — preserving proprioceptive demand is
important for long-term foot/ankle strength.

## 6. Running Form Cues

### Key Biomechanical Cues (evidence-based)

| Cue | Target | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **Cadence** | 170–180 spm (recreational) | Reduces overstriding and impact loading |
| **Foot landing** | Under the hips, not ahead of body | Reduces braking impulse; decreases patellofemoral load |
| **Forward lean** | ~5° from ankle (not waist) | Recruits posterior chain, reduces heel strike |
| **Arm swing** | 90° elbow, drives forward/back not across | Counterbalances leg action, reduces rotation waste |
| **Hip extension** | Drive heel toward glute at toe-off | Maximizes propulsion from glute/hamstring |
| **Glute activation** | Conscious squeeze at stance | Stabilizes pelvis; reduces knee valgus |

### Cadence and Load
- Increasing cadence by 5–10% reduces impact peak forces ~14% and knee load ~20%
- **Metronome apps:** Garmin Running Cadence, Metronome Beats (170–180 bpm = 85–90 per foot)
- Don't increase cadence abruptly — add 2–3 spm per week

### Overstriding
Overstriding (foot landing >30cm ahead of COM) is the most modifiable injury risk factor:
- Leads to high braking forces → patellofemoral stress, stress fractures, shin splints
- Cue: "land under your hips" or "shorten your stride, quicken your feet"
- Video yourself from the side at your easy training pace

## 7. Training Load Management

### The 80/20 Rule (Polarized Training)
80% of runs at easy/conversational pace (Zone 1–2); 20% at moderate-hard (Zone 3–5).
This applies across recreational to elite runners.

### Defining Easy Pace
- Conversational pace — can speak in full sentences
- HR Zone 2: 60–70% HRmax
- RPE 3–4/10
- "Comfortable, could hold for hours"

### Load Progression Rules

**10% Rule:** Increase weekly volume by no more than 10% per week.
While not perfectly evidence-based, it provides a useful guideline for managing overuse risk.

**Better metric — Acute:Chronic Workload Ratio (ACWR):**
```
ACWR = acute load (this week) / chronic load (4-week rolling average)

Safe zone:    0.8 – 1.3
Risk zone:    > 1.5 (injury risk increases significantly)
```
Use Training Peaks, Garmin Connect, or Strava's fitness graph to track load.

### Shoe Rotation for Load Management
Rotating 2–3 pairs of shoes with different specs is evidence-supported for injury reduction:
- Distributes mechanical loading across different tissues
- Different drops load Achilles and knee differently
- Different stack heights vary proprioceptive demand

**Example rotation:**
| Day | Shoe | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Easy runs | HOKA Bondi 9 or NB 1080 v15 | Max cushion, reduce fatigue |
| Tempo/workouts | Saucony Endorphin Speed or NB Rebel v4 | Responsive, efficient |
| Long runs | HOKA Clifton 9 or Brooks Glycerin 22 | Balanced cushion + durability |
| Race | Carbon plate shoe | Maximal performance |

## 8. Common Running Injuries — Shoe Reasoning

### Plantar Fasciitis (PF)
**Mechanism:** Overload of plantar fascia at calcaneal insertion.
**Risk factors:** Low arch, sudden mileage increase, worn shoes, tight calf/Achilles.
**Shoe considerations:**
- Higher drop (10–12mm) reduces Achilles pull on plantar fascia
- Arch support and cushioned heel
- Good options: Brooks Adrenaline GTS, ASICS Kayano, NB 860 v15

### Patellofemoral Pain Syndrome (Runner's Knee)
**Mechanism:** Excessive compression or shear force at patellofemoral joint.
**Risk factors:** Hip weakness, knee valgus, overstriding, Q-angle.
**Shoe considerations:**
- Lower drop may reduce knee flexion moment
- Stability shoe if knee valgus is present
- Increase cadence (reduces peak knee load)
- Good options: HOKA Gaviota, Brooks Adrenaline GTS, reduce drop gradually

### Achilles Tendinopathy
**Mechanism:** Reactive or degenerative tendinopathy from load spikes.
**Risk factors:** Sudden drop reduction, hill running, speed increase.
**Shoe considerations:**
- Temporarily INCREASE drop (12mm) to offload tendon
- Avoid low-drop shoes during acute phase
- Eccentric heel drop exercises (Alfredson protocol)
- Good options: Brooks Ghost 17, ASICS GT-2000 13, NB 860 v15

### IT Band Syndrome (ITBS)
**Mechanism:** Friction/compression of IT band at lateral knee.
**Risk factors:** Hip weakness, leg length discrepancy, cambered roads, overtraining.
**Shoe considerations:**
- Shoes do not directly cause ITBS but worn/overpronated shoes may contribute
- Avoid highly cambered roads (run on even surfaces)
- Hip strengthening is primary intervention

### Stress Fractures (tibia, metatarsals)
**Mechanism:** Bone fatigue from repetitive impact without adequate recovery.
**Risk factors:** Low bone density, rapid mileage increase, nutritional deficiency, hard surfaces.
**Shoe considerations:**
- Higher stack/more cushioned shoe to attenuate peak impacts
- Avoid hard surfaces during return-to-run
- HOKA Bondi 9 or NB 1080 v15 for high cushion during return

### Shin Splints (MTSS — Medial Tibial Stress Syndrome)
**Mechanism:** Periosteal stress at posteromedial tibia.
**Risk factors:** Overpronation, rapid mileage increase, worn shoes.
**Shoe considerations:**
- Stability shoe if overpronation present (NB 860, Brooks Adrenaline)
- Replace shoes at or before 700–800km / 500 miles
- Gradual mileage ramp

## 9. Shoe Selection Framework

### Step 1 — Identify the Runner Profile
```
Gait:          neutral / mild overpronator / moderate overpronator / supinator
Build:         light (<65kg) / medium (65–85kg) / heavy (>85kg)
Surface:       road / treadmill / trail / mixed
Goal:          beginner / recreational / marathon / ultra / performance
Weekly km:     <30 / 30–60 / 60–90 / >90
```

### Step 2 — Map to Shoe Category
| Runner profile | Start here |
|---|---|
| Neutral gait, easy runs | Neutral, high-moderate cushion (Ghost, Clifton, 880) |
| Neutral gait, high mileage | Neutral max-cushion (1080, Bondi, Nimbus) |
| Moderate overpronator | Stability moderate-cushion (Adrenaline GTS, GT-2000, 860) |
| Severe overpronator | Stability max-cushion (Kayano, Gaviota) |
| Supinator | Neutral max-cushion with wide base (Bondi, Nimbus) |
| Speed work | Responsive low-stack (Rebel v4, Endorphin Speed) |
| Racing | Carbon plate (SuperComp Elite, Adios Pro, Vaporfly) |
| Low-drop preference | Altra, HOKA Bondi 9 (4mm), NB More v4 (4mm) |
| Zero drop | Altra Torin / Paradigm (with adaptation protocol) |
| Achilles history | High drop (12mm: Ghost, Glycerin, Wave Rider) |

### Step 3 — Validate Against Spec
Use the `lookup_shoe` and `find_shoes_by_spec` tools to retrieve specs and confirm:
- Drop within target range
- Stack sufficient for surface/mileage/weight
- Weight acceptable for use case
- MSRP within budget

### Step 4 — Plan Shoe Rotation
Build a rotation of 2–3 shoes that varies:
- At least one high-cushion day shoe
- At least one more responsive trainer
- Drop variation of ≥4mm across the rotation

## 10. Carbon-Plated Shoes — Correct Use

### What Carbon Plates Do
- Increase midsole longitudinal bending stiffness
- Reduce metatarsophalangeal joint energy cost via stiffness
- Combined with PEBA foam → 4–8% running economy improvement over traditional trainers

### Who Benefits
- Runners with lactate threshold pace <5:00/km for marathons
- Any runner racing or doing target-pace work benefits from energy savings
- Beginners benefit less (gait efficiency gains are smaller)

### Training Use — Cautions
- Carbon shoes are not designed for daily training mileage
- PEBA foams degrade faster than EVA (300–500km vs 700–800km)
- Reserve for races and key workout sessions
- Use a plated trainer (Endorphin Speed, Rebel v4) for workout days → saves carbon shoes

### Injury Considerations
- Carbon plate increases MTP joint stiffness — may worsen hallux rigidus
- Aggressive stack + plate geometry → Achilles overload in unconditioned runners
- Transition to carbon-plated super shoes gradually (as with any major shoe change)
