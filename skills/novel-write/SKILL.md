# Novel Writing — Drafting Phase Skill

*Part of the novel-writing skill suite. See also `/skill:novel-plan` and `/skill:novel-review`.*

---

## The Prime Directive of Drafting

**Write forward. Never edit backward.**

The first draft is raw material — not the novel. Its job is to exist, not to be good. A complete messy draft is infinitely more valuable than a polished chapter 1 you've rewritten forty times. Editing a blank page is impossible. Editing a complete draft is just work.

Every instinct to go back and fix will cost you momentum. Note the fix needed (a comment, a bracketed `[FIX THIS]`), and keep moving.

---

## The Scene as the Unit of Work

Every scene — every single one — needs three things:

### Goal
What does the POV character want in this scene? Not vaguely ("she wants things to be better") but specifically and immediately ("she wants Marcus to agree not to tell their father about the money").

If you don't know the scene's goal before you write it, stop. Find the goal. A scene without a goal is a sequence of events, not a scene.

### Conflict
What prevents the character from getting the goal? Conflict is not argument — it's friction, obstacle, opposition. It can be:
- Another character with an opposing goal
- An external circumstance (the storm, the locked door, the deadline)
- The protagonist's own Lie, wound, or avoidance behaviour (often the most powerful)

### Turn
How does the scene end differently from how it began? Something must change. Options:
- The character gets what they wanted (but at a cost, or creating a new problem)
- The character fails to get what they wanted (and the situation is now worse)
- The character gets something completely different from what they wanted (and must now reckon with it)

**A scene where nothing changes should be cut, compressed into narration, or merged with an adjacent scene.**

> **Practise the scene equation:** *[Character] wants [goal] but [conflict] and so [turn].*
> If you can't fill in all four, the scene isn't ready to write.

## Daily Practice

### The Only Rule That Matters

Consistency compounds. Write every day if you can. The number is less important than the habit.

| Target | Novel length | At pace |
|--------|-------------|---------|
| 250 words/day | 80,000 words | ~320 days |
| 500 words/day | 80,000 words | ~160 days |
| 1,000 words/day | 80,000 words | ~80 days |
| 2,000 words/day | 80,000 words | ~40 days |

**250 words is one page.** Anyone can write one page. The target should feel slightly too easy — you will often exceed it, but you will never miss it due to the size of the commitment.

### The Stop Technique (Hemingway's Method)
Stop each writing session mid-sentence, mid-scene, or at a moment where you know exactly what comes next. Never stop at the end of a chapter or section — that's where the blank page waits. Leave yourself a running start.

### The Daily Note
Before closing each session, write one sentence about what happens *next*. Not the whole plan — just the next beat. It costs 30 seconds and eliminates the paralysis of the blank start.

### Session Ritual
- Reread only the last 300 words of yesterday's session (enough to get the voice back without triggering editing)
- Set a timer (25–45 min sessions work better than open-ended marathons)
- Silence notifications
- Write

## POV — Point of View Consistency

### Choosing POV Before Drafting

| POV | Effect | Best for |
|-----|--------|----------|
| First person (`I`) | Immediate intimacy; reader is *in* the character | Character-driven literary fiction, romance, thriller |
| Third limited (`She/He/They`, one character's interiority at a time) | Intimacy + flexibility; most common in commercial fiction | Almost any genre |
| Third omniscient | God-view narrator; can know anything, any character | Epic fantasy, historical fiction, literary fiction |
| Second person (`You`) | Unsettling, immersive, experimental | Literary; choose-your-own-adventure; rare |

### POV Discipline in the Draft

**One POV per scene.** Avoid head-hopping — jumping between characters' interiority within a single scene. Pick the character who has the most to lose in this scene, or who is changed most by it. That's your POV.

**Everything filtered through the POV character's perception:**
- Descriptions are *their* descriptions — shaped by their psychology, history, knowledge
- They cannot know what other characters are thinking or feeling (only infer)
- They cannot see themselves — only what they do and how others react

**POV voice consistency:** The prose texture should feel different when in different characters' heads. Their vocabulary, what they notice, how they interpret events — all shaped by who they are.

## Dialogue Craft

### The Invisible Attribution Rule
`Said` is invisible. Readers skip past it. Use it as the default. Exotic said-substitutes (*exclaimed, ejaculated, opined, retorted*) call attention to themselves and interrupt the scene.

Use *asked* for questions. Use action beats for everything else.

### Action Beats Over Dialogue Tags
Action beats replace tags while adding physical grounding:
- `"I won't go," she said.` → `"I won't go." She crossed her arms.`
- `"I know," he said quietly.` → `"I know." He looked out the window.`

Action beats anchor the reader in the physical scene during conversation, preventing dialogue from floating in white space.

### What Dialogue Is Not
Dialogue is not transcribed real conversation. Real conversation is full of filler, repetition, politeness conventions, and topic drift. Fictional dialogue is compressed, purposeful, and subtext-heavy.

**Every line of dialogue should do at least one of:**
- Advance the plot
- Reveal character
- Carry subtext
- Shift the dynamic between characters

If it does none of these, cut it.

### Subtext
Characters rarely say exactly what they mean. The gap between what's said and what's meant is where dramatic electricity lives.

> *"You don't have to stay."*
> *"I know."*
> *He stayed.*

Three lines. No explicit emotion. Everything in what's not said.

### Contractions
Use them. Characters in conversation use contractions. `I am not going to do this` is formal register — appropriate if the character is being stiff or formal as a choice, not as a default. `I'm not going to do this` is natural.

## Exposition and Worldbuilding in the Draft

### The Three Rules of Exposition

1. **Never explain what can be shown.** Characters living in their world don't explain it to each other. They react to it, navigate it, complain about it — but they don't lecture about it.
2. **Deliver exposition in the service of a scene goal.** The moment to explain the magic system is when the protagonist *needs* it to do something and discovers the limits. The moment to reveal backstory is when a character *needs* it to make a decision.
3. **One new concept at a time.** Don't introduce three new fantasy elements in one paragraph. Readers build mental models incrementally — overwhelm produces skipping.

### The "As You Know, Bob" Problem
The character who explains things another character already knows is the writer talking to the reader, badly disguised. Readers see through it instantly.

**Fix:** Find a character who genuinely doesn't know. Or present the information through discovery, conflict, or dramatic irony (reader knows something character doesn't).

### Backstory
Backstory earns its place when it changes how the reader understands the present scene — not as context, but as revelation. Present the scene first. Earn the backstory. Drop it in where it lands with maximum impact.

## Writing the Hard Scenes

### Action Scenes
- **Short sentences create speed.** Matching form to content is not a gimmick; it's technique.
- **Keep the reader oriented.** In choreographed action, readers lose spatial tracking easily. Re-establish who is where every few beats.
- **Every action has a consequence.** Characters get tired, hurt, and scared. Action without cost is video game logic.
- **The emotional core of the scene:** What is your protagonist *feeling* under the adrenaline? That's the scene beneath the scene.

### Intimate Scenes (Sensual to Explicit)
See `/skill:novel-craft` for the full framework. Key reminders for drafting:

- **Know your heat level before writing the scene.** It determines vocabulary, what to show, and how explicit to be.
- **The emotional purpose first.** What does this scene reveal or shift that couldn't happen any other way?
- **Write it fast and messy in the first draft.** Intimate scenes are terrible when over-worked at draft stage — the self-consciousness shows. Draft it quickly; refine in revision.
- **Sexual tension is often better than the act.** Consider whether a charged scene stopping *before* resolution serves the story better than crossing the threshold.

### Exposition-Heavy Scenes (Worldbuilding, Backstory)
- Give the exposition to a character with a **stake in the information.** Exposition from a character who cares creates drama; from a neutral explainer, it creates textbooks.
- **Cut the opening 10%.** Most exposition scenes start before the real starting point. The first page of an exposition scene is usually the author warming up. Cut it.
- **Dramatise the emotion, not the facts.** Readers remember how information made them feel, not the information itself.

## When the Outline Breaks Down

The outline is a hypothesis. The draft tests it. When the story surprises you:

### Diagnostic Questions

1. **Is this surprise better than what I planned?** Sometimes the subconscious surfaces something truer. Follow it. Fix the outline to match.
2. **Is this surprise a distraction or an avoidance?** Sometimes the story "wants" to go somewhere because the writer is avoiding the hard scene ahead. Identify the hard scene. Write it.
3. **Have I earned this surprise for the reader?** A character revelation that comes from nowhere is a cheap twist. If the surprise is right, plant it earlier in revision.

### The Stuck Scene Protocol

When you cannot write the current scene:
1. Write `[PLACEHOLDER: what happens here]` and skip to the next scene you can write
2. Or write the scene from a different character's POV
3. Or write the scene in the wrong genre (make it a comedy; make it a horror sequence) — breaks the block, often reveals what's wrong
4. Or step back: does the scene need to exist? Is it doing the Goal/Conflict/Turn work?

### Mid-Draft Slump (The Muddy Middle)

The middle of Act 2 (roughly 30–60% through) is the most commonly abandoned point. The excitement of starting is gone; the relief of finishing is not yet visible.

**Causes:**
- Protagonist too passive — reacting to events rather than driving them
- Midpoint wasn't impactful enough — stakes didn't genuinely shift
- Subplots have drifted from the theme

**Fixes:**
- Give the protagonist a proactive goal in every scene (not just reacting)
- Revisit the Midpoint — did something genuinely irreversible happen?
- Identify the All Is Lost moment (75% mark) and write backward toward it

## The Drafting Mindset

**Permission to be bad.** The first draft is between you and the document. No one reads it in this state. The internal critic is correct: the draft is bad. That's correct and irrelevant — the critic's job is revision, not drafting. Silence it during the forward movement.

**Trust the structure.** If you planned well, the outline pulls you forward. If the scene isn't working, check the Goal/Conflict/Turn before concluding the plan is wrong.

**Write past the ending of the scene.** When you're close to the scene's end and you don't know exactly how it resolves, write three different endings. Choose the best one in revision.

**The draft is thinking.** Writing a scene you later cut is not wasted work — it's how you discovered what the scene should actually be. All first draft scenes are hypothesis scenes.

## Checkpoints (Run These Mid-Draft)

At the 25% mark:
- [ ] Is your protagonist pursuing a clear goal?
- [ ] Have you established their Lie?
- [ ] Has the Catalyst happened and forced a commitment?

At the 50% mark:
- [ ] Has the Midpoint landed? Did something irreversible shift?
- [ ] Is the protagonist now proactive (not just reactive)?
- [ ] Does the B Story (relationship/theme subplot) feel present?

At the 75% mark:
- [ ] Has the All Is Lost hit?
- [ ] Does it feel like the worst possible version of your protagonist's fear?
- [ ] Can you see the path to the Break Into Three?

## Finishing the Draft

The last 20% is where most writers slow down because they don't want it to end (or they're afraid the ending won't live up to the setup).

**Write the ending fast.** The ending has been earned by everything before it. Trust that. Draft it fast and at full commitment. You can pull it back in revision; you cannot add impact that isn't there.

**The final image.** The last image of the book should mirror the first — same or similar setup, visibly different character. Show the transformation through a specific, concrete image. Don't explain it. Show it. Let the reader feel the distance traveled.

**After the draft:** Do not revise immediately. Put it in a drawer for at least two weeks (four is better). You need temporal distance to see it clearly. During this time, read widely, make notes, but don't open the manuscript.
