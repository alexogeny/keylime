# Novel Review — Revision Phase Skill

*Part of the novel-writing skill suite. See also `/skill:novel-plan` and `/skill:novel-write`.*
*For detailed prose rules and genre standards, see `/skill:novel-craft`.*

---

## The Revision Mindset

**Revision is where the novel is born.** The first draft is raw material. The revision is the creative act of finding the novel inside it.

**The hierarchy is inviolable:**
1. Structural revision first
2. Scene revision second
3. Prose revision third
4. Line edit fourth
5. Proofread last

**Do not polish sentences in scenes you haven't confirmed will survive.** If you write beautiful prose in a scene that gets cut in structural revision, you've wasted the time and — worse — made the scene harder to cut because you've fallen in love with it.

**Temporal distance first.** If you've just finished the draft: put it away for a minimum of 2 weeks. 4 weeks is better. Return to it as a reader, not as the writer who created it. This distance is not optional — it's the single most powerful revision tool available.

---

## Pass 1 — Structural Revision

Read the full manuscript before making a single change. Read as a reader, not as the writer. Make notes in the margin; do not edit in pass 1. Ask only structural questions.

### 1.1 Character Arc Audit

For each major character (protagonist + antagonist + major secondaries):

- [ ] What is the protagonist's Lie at the start? Is it visible to the reader in Act 1?
- [ ] Does the plot force the protagonist to act from the Lie repeatedly, until it fails?
- [ ] Is there a clear moment where the protagonist recognises the Truth (Break Into Three)?
- [ ] Does the final image show the character *after* the arc? Is the transformation visible?
- [ ] Is the transformation earned, or does it happen arbitrarily?

**Red flags:**
- Protagonist doesn't change (unless intentional flat arc — and you chose that)
- Protagonist changes but there's no clear moment it happens
- The internal arc (Lie/Truth) and external arc (plot conflict) don't rhyme — they should be two versions of the same story

### 1.2 Plot Structure Audit

Map your actual story against your chosen framework (STC beats, Three-Act, or your outline):

| Beat | Planned position | Actual position | Notes |
|------|-----------------|-----------------|-------|
| Catalyst | ~10% | | |
| Break Into Two | ~20% | | |
| Midpoint | ~50% | | |
| All Is Lost | ~75% | | |
| Break Into Three | ~80% | | |

**Questions:**
- [ ] Does the Catalyst actually break the ordinary world, or does the protagonist delay too long?
- [ ] Does the Midpoint genuinely shift the stakes, or does it just continue the same throughline?
- [ ] Does the All Is Lost feel like the worst version of the protagonist's fear?
- [ ] Is the resolution driven by character change, or by a convenient plot device?

### 1.3 Pacing Audit

Lay out chapters visually. Note which are:
- **Action/event** (plot moves)
- **Reaction/reflection** (character processes)
- **Dialogue/interpersonal** (relationship dynamics)
- **Worldbuilding/exposition** (setting, backstory)

**Red flags:**
- 3+ consecutive action chapters: readers burn out; insert a reaction scene
- 3+ consecutive reflection chapters: story stalls; insert an event
- All exposition concentrated in Act 1: distribute it across the novel, delivered when needed
- Second act uniformly paced: the middle needs acceleration toward the All Is Lost

### 1.4 Scene Function Audit

For every scene: does it do the Goal/Conflict/Turn work?

Scan quickly: create a one-line summary of each scene in a document. For each one ask:

1. What does the POV character want in this scene?
2. What prevents them getting it?
3. What changes by the end?

If you can't answer all three → the scene likely needs to be cut, compressed, or merged.

**Scenes that survive this test and still feel slow:** check whether they're doing the work through the most interesting possible execution. The scene's function is right, but the *manner* is dull.

### 1.5 Subplot Audit

List all subplots and where they appear:
- Does each subplot thread appear with enough regularity that readers don't forget it?
- Does each subplot have its own arc (beginning, complication, resolution)?
- Does each subplot rhyme with the theme — or is it orphaned filler?
- Do any subplots resolve before they've earned resolution?

## Pass 2 — Scene-Level Revision

With structural issues resolved, work through the manuscript scene by scene. Focus on *how* each scene executes its function.

### 2.1 Opening Line / Scene Hook

Does the first line of each scene create a question in the reader's mind? The question doesn't have to be dramatic — it just has to make the reader curious about what comes next.

**Test:** Cover the opening line. Read from line 2. Does the reader miss the first line? If not, the first line isn't earning its position. Consider starting the scene at the real starting point — most scenes begin one paragraph too early.

### 2.2 Scene Anchoring Check

Within the first 3-5 lines of every scene: has the reader been given WHO, WHERE, WHEN?

### 2.3 Dialogue Pass (Scene Level)

Read dialogue scenes aloud. Listen for:
- Characters who all sound the same (same vocabulary, rhythm, sentence length)
- Dialogue that's just plot delivery (`"As you know, Bob..."`)
- Absence of subtext — characters saying exactly what they mean, every time
- Tags and beats: are beats varied and physical? Or is it all `she said`/`he said`?
- Contractions: does dialogue sound natural when spoken?

Each major character should have a distinct spoken voice. This comes from:
- Vocabulary range (educated/casual/technical register)
- Sentence length and complexity
- What they avoid saying
- What they say instead of what they mean

### 2.4 POV Consistency Check

For each scene:
- [ ] Is the POV clear from the first few lines?
- [ ] Are there any points where the narrative enters another character's head (head-hopping)?
- [ ] Does every description and observation sound like it's coming from the POV character — their psychology, not the author's?

### 2.5 Emotional Beats

Is the emotional core of each scene visible? Not stated — *felt*.

Test: mark every place in the scene where you explicitly *tell* the reader what the character feels. For each: could this be shown through action, dialogue, or physical detail instead? Convert where possible.

---

## Pass 3 — Prose Revision

With structure and scenes confirmed, work at the sentence level. This is the pass that makes the prose shine — but it's the last craft pass, not the first.

### 3.1 Filter Word Pass

Search your manuscript for each filter word. Interrogate every instance:
```
heard    saw     felt    noticed    realised    watched
observed thought wondered perceived  sensed
```

For each: can cutting the filter word and rephrasing give the reader more direct access to the scene?

**Before:** `She felt her chest tighten when she noticed the window was open.`
**After:** `Her chest tightened. The window was open.`

### 3.2 Crutch Word Pass

Search and thin out instances:
```
just   really   very   suddenly   began to   started to
almost  quite   rather  seemed    appeared   like (stacked similes)
```

### 3.3 Purple Prose Pass

Find every passage that does more describing than moving. Ask:
- How many metaphors are on this one image? (More than one → cut to the strongest)
- Is anything happening here, or is it pure description? (If pure description → cut 50% minimum)
- Does this passage sound like the author showing off, or the character experiencing? (If the former → rewrite from character POV)
- Read aloud: does it feel melodramatic? (If yes → reduce one layer of intensity)

### 3.4 Adverb Pass

Search for `ly ` (adverb + space). For each adverb modifying a verb:
- Can the adverb be eliminated by choosing a stronger verb?
- `walked quickly` → `strode`; `said angrily` → `snapped`; `cried quietly` → `wept`

### 3.5 Passive Voice Pass

Search for: `was`, `were`, `been`, `being` followed by a past participle.
- Is this passive voice? If so, is it intentional?
- If accidental → convert to active

### 3.6 Repetition Pass

- **Crutch words:** Run a word frequency count. Any non-function word appearing with unusual frequency → replace some instances
- **Synonymic repetition:** Same sensory beat, different words, within 3 pages → keep one
- **Chapter opening repetition:** Read first lines of all chapters consecutively. Same pattern 3+ times → vary

### 3.7 Sentence Rhythm Pass

Read sections aloud. Listen for:
- Uniform sentence length (monotony) → vary long/short
- Sentences that all start with subject-verb → rotate the structure
- Passages that feel rushed (too many short sentences) or sluggish (all long)
- Where does the reader's breath fall naturally? Use that.

## Pass 4 — Line Edit

Micro-level precision. Work slowly — a few pages per session.

### Line Edit Checklist (Per Paragraph)

- [ ] Does every sentence earn its place? (If cutting it loses nothing → cut it)
- [ ] Is every word the most precise available? (Weak noun + modifier → single strong noun)
- [ ] Are there any redundancies? (`She looked up at the sky` → `She looked at the sky`)
- [ ] Any doubled intensifiers? (`very extremely cold` → `brutal`)
- [ ] Dangling modifiers? (`Running down the street, the rain soaked her` → who is running?)
- [ ] Unclear pronoun references? (`She told her that she thought her idea was bad` — who is who?)
- [ ] Consistent tense throughout each scene?
- [ ] Consistent POV throughout each scene?

### The Cut Test

For every paragraph: what happens if you delete it entirely? If the answer is "nothing changes" — delete it. If the answer is "the reader loses something essential" — keep it. If the answer is "the reader loses context that could live somewhere better" — move it.

---

## Pass 5 — Proofread

Final pass. Fix only errors, not craft. This is not the place for rewrites.

- [ ] Spelling (use a spell-checker *and* read manually — spell-checkers miss correctly-spelled wrong words)
- [ ] Grammar and punctuation
- [ ] Consistency: character names, physical descriptions, place names, timeline
- [ ] Chapter/section numbering
- [ ] Dialogue punctuation (commas inside quotes before tags, periods at sentence end)
- [ ] Em-dashes vs en-dashes vs hyphens used consistently
- [ ] Formatting: paragraph indent or line-break (not both), consistent throughout

## The Complete Revision Checklist

### Structural
- [ ] Protagonist Lie established in Act 1
- [ ] Lie tested and failed through plot events
- [ ] Truth discovered at Break Into Three
- [ ] Final Image shows transformation
- [ ] Catalyst at ~10%; Midpoint at ~50%; All Is Lost at ~75%
- [ ] Every scene has Goal / Conflict / Turn
- [ ] Scenes that fail the GCT test have been cut or revised
- [ ] Pacing varied (action / reaction / dialogue / exposition)
- [ ] All subplots thread through with consistent presence

### Scene Level
- [ ] Opening line of each scene creates forward pull
- [ ] WHO/WHERE/WHEN anchored in first 3-5 lines of each scene
- [ ] Dialogue sounds distinct per character; subtext present
- [ ] No head-hopping within scenes
- [ ] Emotional beats shown, not just told
- [ ] Scenes begin at the real starting point (no slow warm-up paragraphs)

### Prose Level
- [ ] Filter words hunted and cut
- [ ] Crutch words thinned
- [ ] Purple prose reduced
- [ ] Adverbs replaced with strong verbs
- [ ] Passive voice made intentional
- [ ] Repetition (synonymic, structural, character name) addressed
- [ ] Sentence rhythm varied

### Line Edit
- [ ] Every sentence earns its place
- [ ] Redundancies removed
- [ ] Pronoun references clear
- [ ] Tense consistent per scene
- [ ] POV consistent per scene

### Proofread
- [ ] Spelling
- [ ] Grammar and punctuation
- [ ] Internal consistency (names, descriptions, timeline)
- [ ] Formatting

## Getting External Feedback

Before submitting to agents/editors or publishing, get at least one reader who:
- Reads in your genre (knows the conventions and reader contract)
- Will tell you what doesn't work, not just what they liked
- Has no personal stake in sparing your feelings

**What to ask beta readers:**
1. At what point did you lose interest or feel the pace drop? (Structural feedback)
2. Was there any character you didn't care about? (Character feedback)
3. Was there anything confusing — plot, worldbuilding, or character motivation? (Clarity feedback)
4. What did you want more of? Less of? (Reader experience feedback)

Do NOT ask: "Was it good?" — this produces unusable answers.

**On feedback:** Not all feedback is correct. But feedback that recurs from multiple independent readers almost always is. If three beta readers trip on the same thing, it's a problem regardless of whether you intended it.

---

## When to Stop Revising

Revision is never truly finished — only abandoned at a point of diminishing returns.

Stop when:
- You're no longer improving the manuscript; you're just changing it
- You're making changes, then reverting them in the next pass
- The remaining issues are matters of taste, not craft errors

At that point: it's done. Ship it.
