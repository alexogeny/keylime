# Genre Deep-Dives — Novel Craft Reference

## Science Fiction

### What Makes Great Sci-Fi

Great science fiction is always **about the present, disguised as the future**. Technology and science are the lens, not the subject.

**The "What If" Engine:**
Start from a single, specific, novel premise and follow it ruthlessly to its logical conclusion. Don't hedge. Don't soften. Don't split the difference.
- *1984*: What if surveillance became the architecture of totalitarianism?
- *Dune*: What if ecological scarcity was the engine of imperial politics?
- *The Left Hand of Darkness*: What if gender didn't exist — what would that reveal about human society?

**Internal Consistency (Non-Negotiable):**
Your future/alternate universe has rules. You obey them. If you violate them, you need a *convincing in-world reason* — not authorial convenience. Readers of sci-fi are specifically attuned to logical violations; they will notice before your editor does.

**Worldbuilding Questions to Answer Before Drafting:**
- What is the dominant technology, and what has it *changed* about human relationships?
- What are the political systems, and how do they relate to that technology?
- What is the key resource, and who controls it?
- What do people believe? (Religion, ideology, cultural norms that replaced old ones)
- What is forbidden, and why?
- What does your protagonist's daily life look like — what's ordinary to *them* that would be extraordinary to us?

**The "As You Know Bob" Problem:**
Characters in a science fiction world don't explain their world to each other — they live in it. Exposition must be delivered through conflict, misunderstanding, discovery, or dramatic irony — never through a character explaining things another character would already know.

**Character Grounded in the Extraordinary:**
Your protagonist must treat the extraordinary as ordinary. Starship pilots don't marvel at starships. They worry about fuel costs and crew drama. Ground the extraordinary through the specific and mundane.

**Sci-Fi Tropes Worth Knowing:**
- First contact (alien encounter and its communication/cultural challenges)
- Dystopia (authoritarian societies; surveillance; ecological collapse)
- Post-apocalypse (rebuilding; survival; what humanity retains)
- Space opera (large-scale, epic; political intrigue across star systems)
- Cyberpunk (tech-saturated inequality; corporate power; identity vs. body)
- Hard sci-fi (scientifically rigorous; real physics; near-future plausibility)
- Solarpunk (optimistic; ecological abundance; alternative futures)

**Common Sci-Fi Failures:**
- Technology that functions as magic (no rules, no costs)
- Ignoring the social consequences of the central premise
- Exposition dumps in chapter 1 explaining how the world works
- The "chosen one" framing transplanted from fantasy without sci-fi grounding
- Near-future settings that don't account for how AI/climate/biotech have already changed the present

## Fantasy

### What Makes Great Fantasy

**Internal Consistency:**
The rules of your world must be applied consistently. Magic, cosmology, political structures, biology — once established, they govern the story. The reader builds a model of your world as they read; every violation of that model breaks their trust.

**Character Transformation Through Quest:**
Settings, battles, and magic are backdrop. What matters is what the quest *does to the hero*. The transformation must be:
- **Visible** — readers see it happening through action and choice, not narration
- **Costly** — transformation without loss is wish-fulfilment, not story
- **Permanent** — the hero cannot fully return to who they were in page 1

**Sanderson's Three Laws of Magic** (the essential framework — load this carefully):

**First Law:** An author's ability to solve conflict with magic is directly proportional to how well the reader understands said magic.
- Soft magic (mysterious, unexplained — Gandalf, the Force) creates wonder. But you CANNOT use it to save the day. That's a deus ex machina and destroys reader trust.
- Hard magic (clear rules, known limits — Mistborn's Allomancy, Avatar's bending) CAN be used to solve problems cleverly, because the reader can track the solution.
- The middle path: use magic to solve problems only when it's clear to the reader that solving this problem this way was always possible.

**Second Law:** The limitations and costs of magic are more interesting than its powers.
- What CAN'T the magic do? What does using it cost? What are the rules the character cannot break?
- A character who can do anything is boring. A character who can do almost anything but at terrible cost, or only under specific conditions, is compelling.
- Examples of powerful limitations: magic depletes the user's lifespan (Fullmetal Alchemist's equivalent exchange); magic requires a physical price (Mistborn); magic requires rare materials; magic attracts dangerous attention.

**Third Law:** Expand what you already have before adding something new.
- Develop the full implications of your existing system before introducing a second one.
- Depth beats breadth. Readers want to master one system, not track six.

**Magic System Design Starting Points:**
- What is the source? (Internal — the user; External — nature, gods, objects)
- What is the cost? (Physical, mental, moral, social, temporal)
- What are the limits? (Scope, precision, reach, frequency)
- What does it look like? (Visual, sensory — magic must be visible and trackable)
- Who can use it? (Everyone, rare individuals, trained practitioners, bloodlines)

**Worldbuilding Depth:**
Tolkien spent decades building languages, cosmologies, and histories before *The Lord of the Rings* was written. You don't need that depth to start — but you need to know:
- The history that shaped the present political situation
- The mythology your characters believe (even if it's false)
- What the world *was* before the story begins — because your characters live in the aftermath

**Fantasy Tropes Worth Knowing:**
- Chosen One (use carefully — must subvert or execute with extraordinary characterisation)
- The Quest (team assembled; obstacles escalate; transformation through trials)
- Dark Lord / External Evil (works best when mirrored by internal evil in protagonist)
- Hidden Heritage (protagonist discovers secret origin)
- Found Family (group forged through shared ordeal)
- Portal Fantasy (ordinary person enters magical world — fish-out-of-water provides exposition vehicle)

**Common Fantasy Failures:**
- Magic that appears only when the plot needs it, ignoring established rules
- Chosen One protagonist who is the most powerful person in the world from page 1 (no room to grow)
- Exposition chapters explaining world history before readers care about characters
- Vague, inconsistent magic used as a plot convenience
- Heroes who suffer no permanent consequences

## Romance

### The Genre Contract

Romance has the strictest genre contract of any major category:

1. **HEA (Happily Ever After) OR HFN (Happy For Now)** — the romantic relationship must resolve positively. This is not optional. Killing the couple, separating them permanently, or leaving the relationship unresolved is not subversive — it breaks the reader contract. If you want to do that, you're writing a love *story*, not romance.
2. **The romantic arc is the spine.** Plot can be elaborate. The romance must be the central emotional journey.

### What Makes Romance Work

**Character Flaws That Block Love:**
The protagonist's flaw must be *specifically* connected to why they can't let love in. Not generic flaws ("she's clumsy") but psychological wounds:
- She doesn't trust because her last partner abandoned her → romance tests exactly that distrust
- He believes he's unworthy of love → romance forces him to believe differently
- She's driven to prove herself because her family dismissed her → love requires her to value herself outside of achievement

**Chemistry Built Through Opposition:**
Contrasting personalities generate magnetic conflict. The opposition must be one that *only love can resolve* — not a misunderstanding, but a genuine incompatibility of worldview that the relationship reforges.
- *Pride and Prejudice*: Bennet's wit vs. Darcy's pride → both must change
- *It Happened One Summer*: city socialite vs. gruff fisherman → both worlds are valid; both characters open

**Earned Obstacles:**
Every barrier to the couple getting together must arise from **character**, not from manufactured misunderstanding. Readers tolerate external obstacles (family opposition, different worlds, timing). They resent obstacles that would dissolve with one honest conversation — because that's not a character problem, it's a communication problem the author created for convenience.

**The Dark Moment:**
A near-loss or break that forces both characters to actively *choose* the relationship — not drift into it. Without the dark moment, the resolution feels unearned.

**Tropes as Features:**
Romance tropes are beloved because they reliably generate emotional tension. Using them knowingly while executing them freshly is the craft.
- Enemies-to-lovers
- Forced proximity (stuck together)
- Fake dating / marriage of convenience
- Second chance (reunited after a break)
- Grumpy/sunshine
- One bed
- Forbidden romance

The reader wants to see a familiar setup executed with fresh, specific, emotionally true characters — not a subversion of the trope.

### Romantasy (Fantasy-Romance Hybrid) — The Dominant Genre of 2024–2026

Romantasy has exploded from niche to mainstream (driven by *Fourth Wing*, *A Court of Thorns and Roses*, *Radiance*).

**Key distinction:**
- **Fantasy Romance:** Romance is the A-plot; fantasy is the setting. Romance conventions dominate — HEA is the primary obligation.
- **Romantic Fantasy:** Fantasy is the A-plot; romance is the B-story. Epic fantasy conventions dominate.

**Execution rule:** Build the fantasy world first, then layer the romance. Don't try to develop both simultaneously from scratch — the worldbuilding logic must generate the romantic conflict naturally.

**For series:** Map both arcs across the planned books separately. The fantasy arc (e.g. save the kingdom) and the romance arc (e.g. two people falling in love) can resolve at different rates across different books.

## Erotica and Erotic Romance

### The Core Distinction

| | Erotica | Erotic Romance |
|--|---------|----------------|
| Central focus | Sexual experience | Romantic relationship arc |
| HEA/HFN required? | No | Yes |
| Character development | Optional | Essential |
| Plot | Thin to none | Required |
| Reader contract | "Give me heat" | "Give me heat AND love" |

### Heat Level Taxonomy

Choose your heat level before drafting and maintain it consistently. Readers select by heat level — inconsistency breaks trust.

| Level | What's on page |
|-------|---------------|
| Sweet / Closed Door | Fade to black before anything explicit; emotional and romantic focus |
| Sensual | Some physical progression with detail; suggestive language; sensation-forward |
| Steamy | Named body parts, described actions; balanced physical/emotional |
| Explicit | Specific, detailed; anatomy and mechanics front and centre |
| Erotica | Sexual experience as the primary subject; explicit throughout |

### Craft Principles for Intimate Scenes

**1. Emotional foundation first.**
Before characters become physically intimate, readers need to understand *why these specific people* are drawn to each other. What does this person offer that no one else could? What vulnerability does intimacy with this person specifically expose?

**2. The physical is a vehicle for emotional revelation.**
Every intimate scene should do at least one of:
- Shift the power dynamic between characters
- Reveal a vulnerability previously hidden
- Cost one or both characters something (emotionally, psychologically)
- Change the relationship irreversibly

A scene that only depicts physical acts has failed as narrative.

**3. Sexual tension often burns hotter fully clothed.**
The charged conversation. The near-touch that doesn't land. The moment one character almost says something and doesn't. These withhold contact while maximising the reader's desire for it. Master this before writing explicit scenes.

**4. The 12 Stages of Physical Intimacy** (structural tool for building romantic tension across chapters):
Eye-to-body → Eye contact → Voice/conversation → Touch (hand/arm) → Touch (shoulder/back) → Touch (head/hair) → Mouth-to-face → Embrace → Mouth-to-mouth → Touch below waist → Sustained touch → Sexual intimacy

Use these stages to pace your romantic arc across the novel. Skipping stages creates implausible chemistry. Returning to early stages after intimacy creates delicious regression.

**5. Language calibration.**
Two failure modes:
- **Clinical:** "He inserted his penis" — breaks immersion, reads like a medical procedure
- **Euphemistic absurdity:** "her throbbing loins," "his manhood pulsed" — the Bad Sex Award exists for this category
Find language calibrated to your *heat level* AND your *characters' voices*. A biologist and a romance novelist do not use the same words.

**6. Treat intimate scenes like action scenes.**
Same attention to pacing, beat-by-beat momentum, character motivation, and consequences. An action scene where nothing is at stake is boring; so is an intimate scene where nothing is at stake.

## Literary Drama / Character-Driven Fiction

### What Distinguishes It

Literary drama privileges **interiority and relational conflict** over external plot mechanics. The question isn't "what happens?" but "what does what happens *mean* to this person?"

**Where plot comes from:** Character decision under psychological pressure — not external event. Events happen because of who these people *are*, not despite it.

**Ambiguity is permitted.** Not every wound heals. Not every relationship survives. Not every question gets an answer. Readers of literary fiction have made peace with this. Honour it.

### What Makes It Work

**Specificity over generality.**
Literary drama lives and dies by precise, concrete detail. Not "she was sad" but the specific object she picks up — the mug her mother left, still with lipstick on the rim. Not "the house was gloomy" but "the kind of house where you checked over your shoulder before laughing."

**Subtext in dialogue.**
Characters rarely say what they mean. The conversation is *about* one thing; it's *about* another. What goes unsaid carries more weight than what's spoken. Study Pinter, Carver, Alice Munro.

**Earned epiphany.**
The moment of insight or change must feel both inevitable (in retrospect, it could only have ended this way) and surprising (in the moment, it lands as revelation). If the epiphany was visible from chapter 3, it's a spoiler. If it arrives without preparation, it's arbitrary.

**Voice as character.**
In literary fiction, the narrator's voice is often the primary source of pleasure. The way the narrator sees, selects, and frames the world is the novel's argument. Voice must be distinctive, specific, consistent, and must *change* as the protagonist changes.

**The Lyric Mode.**
Some literary drama operates in a lyric rather than dramatic mode — less concerned with what happens, more with the texture of consciousness, memory, and perception. Woolf, Sebald, Anne Carson. This mode requires exceptional prose control; it's the hardest mode to execute and the easiest to write badly (because the failures look like choices).
