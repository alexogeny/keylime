# Prose Rules — Quick Reference Cheatsheet

## The Filter Word Hit-List

Search your manuscript for these. Cut most instances.

```
heard    saw     felt    noticed    realised    watched
observed thought wondered perceived  registered  sensed
```

**Before:** `She heard the door creak and felt her heart race.`
**After:** `The door creaked. Her heart raced.`

---

## The Crutch Word Hit-List

Search and interrogate every instance:

```
just   really   very   suddenly   almost   quite   rather
began  started  seemed appeared   like (simile overuse)
```

**Specific problems:**
- `suddenly` — if it's sudden, the sentence structure will convey that; the word is redundant
- `began to` / `started to` — almost always cut; just do the action (`She began to cry` → `She cried`)
- `seemed` — either it is or it isn't; `seemed` creates false uncertainty that weakens claims

## Strong Verb Substitutions

| Weak verb + adverb | Strong verb |
|--------------------|-------------|
| walked quickly | strode, hurried, bolted, rushed, jogged |
| walked slowly | ambled, shuffled, meandered, plodded |
| said quietly | murmured, whispered, breathed |
| said angrily | snapped, growled, bit out, barked |
| said sadly | sighed, managed, got out |
| looked nervously | darted her eyes, glanced, cut her eyes |
| ran fast | sprinted, hurtled, tore, flew |
| hit hard | slammed, drove, cracked, smashed |
| sat down slowly | lowered herself, sank, folded |
| ate quickly | wolfed, devoured, tore into |

## Show vs. Tell — Common Emotional States

| Telling | Showing |
|---------|---------|
| He was angry. | He set the glass down with a steadiness that cost him something. |
| She was nervous. | She checked her phone twice in thirty seconds. |
| He was exhausted. | He misspelled his own name on the form. |
| She felt guilty. | She watched him pour his own coffee. |
| He was in love. | He remembered what she'd ordered three weeks ago. |
| She was afraid. | She made herself look at the door again. Not yet. |
| He was relieved. | He let out a breath he hadn't tracked taking. |
| She was disgusted. | She smiled anyway. |

**Rule:** Don't tell what you've just shown. Don't show what direct statement serves better. Internal narration often requires direct statement — just never *both* tell and show the same beat.

## Purple Prose Detector

Ask these questions about any description-heavy passage:

1. How many metaphors are stacked on this one thing? → If more than one, cut to the strongest
2. Is anything moving / changing / happening in this passage? → If no, cut 50% of it
3. Could a reader extract the core information in 30% fewer words? → If yes, cut to that
4. Does the voice sound like the *character* or like an author trying to impress? → If the latter, rewrite from character POV
5. Read it aloud. Does it feel melodramatic? → If yes, cut one layer of intensity

**Purple prose specimen:**
> *The sun sank beneath the horizon like a dying ember, painting the sky in cascading waterfalls of crimson and gold, as if the heavens themselves were weeping tears of fire for the day's passing.*

**Cleaned:**
> *The sun went down. The sky went red.*

Sometimes less is vastly more. The second version leaves room for the reader's imagination; the first overstuffs it.

## Passive Voice Audit

**Find passives:** Look for `was/were/been/being` + past participle.

| Passive (accidental) | Active (fix) |
|---------------------|--------------|
| The door was opened by him. | He opened the door. |
| The letter was written quickly. | She wrote the letter quickly. |
| Mistakes were made. | He made mistakes. |
| The coffee was left to cool. | She left the coffee to cool. |

**Keep passive when:**
- The actor is unknown: `The window had been broken.` (we don't know who)
- The actor is irrelevant: `He was born in 1944.` (the birth process isn't the point)
- The acted-upon thing is the focus: `She was struck by the silence.` (the silence, not the striking)

## Dialogue Craft

**Said is invisible.** Readers skip past `said` — use it as the default attribution. Exotic said-substitutes (`he exclaimed`, `she ejaculated`, `he opined`) call attention to themselves.

**Beats over tags.** Action beats before or after dialogue replace tags entirely and add physical grounding:
- `"I can't do this," she said.` → `"I can't do this." She put down the knife.`

**Cut throat-clearing.** Characters don't preface sentences the way people do in conversation:
- `"Well, you see, I've been thinking..."` → `"I've been thinking..."`

**Contractions.** People in dialogue use contractions. `I am not going` is formal register unless there's a reason. `I'm not going` is natural. This matters especially in intimate and emotional scenes.

**Subtext.** What the character says ≠ what they mean. The gap between the two lines is where dramatic tension lives.
> *"How are you?" // "Fine." // "Right." // She poured two drinks.*

## Repetition Audit

Run these checks before submission:

1. **Crutch words:** Search each word on the hit-list above. Flag density clusters.
2. **Character names:** List opening letters. Natalie/Nancy, Tom/Tony = confusion. Change one.
3. **Synonymic repetition:** Find passages where *cold/chilly/icy* or equivalents appear within 3 pages. Keep one.
4. **Scene opening audit:** List first line of every chapter. Same construction 3+ times in a row = fix.
5. **Duplicate characters:** List each character's function. Any two characters serving the same function → merge.

## Sentence Rhythm Reference

**Variation pattern example:**
> *She ran. The ground was wet, the path narrowing where it curved toward the water, and she knew that if she slipped now, in the dark, with no one watching, there would be no recovery. She ran anyway.*

Pattern: Short → Long → Short. The long sentence builds complexity and fear; the short sentences bracket it with decisive action. Read your paragraphs aloud. Monotonous rhythm = edit needed.

**Techniques for rhythm:**
- End sections on short, punchy sentences for impact
- Use long sentences for immersive description and interiority
- Use sentence fragments deliberately: *Not yet. Not here. Not like this.*
- Vary the internal structure: subject-verb-object, verb-subject, prepositional phrase first
