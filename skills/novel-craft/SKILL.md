## The One Rule That Governs Everything

**Prose must serve the story. The moment the reader notices the writing rather than experiencing the story, the prose has failed.**

Every technique below is in service of this principle.

---

## Part 1 — What Good Prose Does

Good prose is **invisible**. Readers should experience the story without noticing the writing. Every sentence earns its place by doing at least one of:
- Advancing plot
- Revealing character
- Establishing or shifting mood
- Delivering information the reader needs to track the story

If a passage does none of these, it belongs in the draft, not the final manuscript.

**The central tension:** clarity vs. lyricism. Genre writers tend toward clarity; literary writers toward lyricism. The masters do both — sentences that are beautiful *and* immediately comprehensible.

## Part 2 — Sentence-Level Craft

### 2.1 Filter Words — The Highest-ROI Edit

Filter words insert the narrator's perception *between* the reader and the action. Cutting them pulls the reader into the scene.

**Filter verbs to search and cut:** *heard, saw, felt, noticed, realised, watched, observed, thought, wondered*

| With filter (distant) | Without filter (immersive) |
|-----------------------|---------------------------|
| She heard footsteps in the hall. | Footsteps in the hall. |
| He noticed the window was open. | The window was open. |
| She felt cold. | The cold settled into her. |
| He realised they were gone. | They were gone. |

The reader is already inside the POV character's head — they don't need to be told the character is perceiving things.

**Exception:** Keep filter words when the act of perception *is* the point — when the character's noticing something is itself revealing.

### 2.2 Strong Verbs Over Adverbs

Adverbs are a diagnostic: they signal a weak verb underneath.

| Adverb version | Strong verb version |
|----------------|---------------------|
| She walked quickly. | She strode / hurried / bolted. |
| He said angrily. | He snapped / growled / bit out. |
| She looked nervously. | She darted her eyes / glanced. |

Search for `-ly` adverbs modifying verbs. Most can be replaced with a single strong verb.

### 2.3 Sentence Rhythm and Variety

**Vary length.** Short sentences punch. Longer sentences carry the reader through accumulated sensation, detail layering on detail, until the sentence itself feels like the thing it describes. Mix them. A page of uniform sentence length becomes a monotonous drumbeat — technically correct, deeply dull.

**Vary openings.** Three consecutive sentences starting with *She* creates structural repetition. Rotate: subject → verb → modifier → phrase openings.

**Rhythm guides pacing:**
- Short sentences = tension, urgency, impact
- Long sentences = reflection, setting, emotional immersion
- One-word sentences. Shock.

### 2.4 Sentence-Level Characterisation

The POV character's personality should shape the *texture* of the writing — not just what they say, but what they notice and how they describe it.

A traumatised soldier moving through a park notices exit routes and sightlines. An architect notices load-bearing structures. A grieving parent notices other people's children. The same street, filtered through three POVs, becomes three different streets.

Practise: Take a neutral scene description. Rewrite it three times through three different character psychologies. The "objective" version is a draft artifact.

### 2.5 Scene Anchoring

Every new scene or chapter needs **WHO, WHERE, WHEN** within the first few lines. Readers may have put the book down. Don't make them guess.

All three can live in a single elegant sentence:
> *Maya hadn't been back to the café since November, but when she pushed through the door on a wet Tuesday in March, it smelled exactly the same.*

That's who (Maya), where (café), and when (March, gap since November) in one line.

## Part 3 — Prose to Avoid

See [references/prose.md](references/prose.md) for the full cheatsheet with examples.

### 3.1 Purple Prose

**Definition:** Over-elaborate, ornate writing that prioritises sound over sense. Writing that calls attention to itself and interrupts the story.

**Hallmarks:**
- Multiple stacked metaphors for a single emotion
- Long lyrical passages where nothing moves or changes
- Melodrama — emotions dramatised past the point of believability
- The author's voice bleeding through the character's POV

**Classic specimen:**
> *"No — please — don't go!" she cried, but the door closed with the final, echoing thud of a guillotine, cutting her off from love, from light, from warmth, from her reason for existence.*

**Fix:** One specific, precise image does more than three ornate ones. *The door closed.* Often that's enough.

**Crucial distinction:** Dense, carefully-considered prose (Nabokov, Hilary Mantel) is NOT purple prose. Purple prose is lazy ornament. Precision and depth are virtues.

### 3.2 Telling Instead of Showing

**Telling** announces emotional state. **Showing** makes the reader feel it themselves.

| Telling | Showing |
|---------|---------|
| He was furious. | He set his glass down with a steadiness that cost him something. |
| She was nervous. | She checked her phone twice in thirty seconds. |
| The house was abandoned. | Paint peeled in curls from the doorframe. A bird had nested in the mailbox. |

**Rule:** Don't tell what you've just shown. Don't tell what you could show more powerfully. Telling is not always wrong — internal narration often requires direct statement — but never *both* tell and show the same beat.

### 3.3 Subtle Repetition

Obvious crutch words (*just, really, very, suddenly*) are easy to find with search. The harder repetition:

- **Synonymic repetition:** Describing a room as *cold*, then *chilly*, then *icy* in separate paragraphs. Three words, one sensory beat. Once = description. Twice = emphasis. Three times = unedited draft.
- **Same word, different meaning:** *The hum of bees* and *the hum of the engine* in close proximity — the brain snags on both.
- **Repetitive scene openings:** Read the first line of each chapter back-to-back. Five consecutive chapters opening with location description = structural monotony.

### 3.4 Clichés

Clichés are invisible to readers — they register as filler, not image. *Heart of gold, cold as ice, face like thunder, time will tell.* Replace with something specific to your character's world and psychology.

### 3.5 Passive Voice (Situational)

Passive voice is not evil — but drains energy from action sentences when used accidentally.

| Passive (weak) | Active (strong) |
|----------------|-----------------|
| The coffee was drunk by her slowly. | She drank her coffee slowly. |
| The door was opened. | He opened the door. |

Use passive voice deliberately: when the actor is unknown, unimportant, or when what's acted upon is the focus.

## Part 4 — Genre Craft

See [references/genres.md](references/genres.md) for full genre breakdowns.

### Quick Genre Summary

| Genre | Reader Contract | Core Engine | Non-Negotiable |
|-------|----------------|-------------|----------------|
| **Sci-fi** | Coherent world; premise followed to its conclusion | "What if" idea + humanity question | Internal consistency; no hand-waving |
| **Fantasy** | Internally consistent magic; hero transformed | Magic system + mythology | Sanderson's Laws; character transformation |
| **Romance** | Emotional journey; couple gets together | Obstacles from character flaw | HEA or HFN ending |
| **Erotica** | Sexual experience is central | Heat, tension, sensation | Heat level maintained; emotional grounding optional per sub-genre |
| **Erotic Romance** | Romance arc + explicit heat | Relationship + sexual tension | HEA/HFN + emotional grounding of intimate scenes |
| **Literary Drama** | Character interiority; ambiguity honoured | Psychological/relational conflict | Specificity; subtext; earned epiphany |

### Sanderson's Three Laws of Magic (Fantasy Essential)

1. **First Law:** Ability to solve conflict with magic ∝ reader's understanding of it. Unexplained (soft) magic cannot save the day — that's a deus ex machina. Explained (hard) magic can be used cleverly.
2. **Second Law:** Limitations and costs are more interesting than powers. Constraints create drama.
3. **Third Law:** Expand existing magic before adding new systems. Depth beats breadth.

### Romance Genre Rules

- **HEA (Happily Ever After) or HFN (Happy For Now)** is non-negotiable. Breaking this is not subversive; it's a genre contract violation.
- Character flaws must be **thematically connected** to why they can't let love in — not arbitrary.
- Obstacles must arise from **character**, not from manufactured misunderstanding that a single honest conversation would dissolve.
- Tropes (enemies-to-lovers, forced proximity, fake dating) are features, not flaws — use knowingly and execute freshly.

### Intimate Scene Craft (Erotica / Erotic Romance)

**Heat level taxonomy — choose one, stay consistent:**

| Level | What's on page |
|-------|---------------|
| Sweet / Closed Door | Fade to black; emotional focus |
| Sensual | Some physical detail; sensation-forward |
| Steamy | Named body parts, described actions; balanced |
| Explicit | Specific, detailed; anatomy/mechanics front and centre |
| Erotica | Sexual experience as primary subject |

**Core principles:**
1. Emotional connection precedes physical intimacy — readers need to understand *why* these specific people are drawn together.
2. The physical is a vehicle for emotional revelation — intimate scenes should shift the relationship or reveal character vulnerability.
3. Sexual tension often burns hotter *fully clothed* — charged dialogue and withheld contact outperform explicit scenes for building tension.
4. Two language failure modes to avoid: *clinical anatomy* ("he inserted his...") and *euphemistic absurdity* ("her throbbing loins"). Find language that fits both your heat level and your characters' voices.
5. Use contractions in dialogue — stilted, formal speech breaks the spell.

## Part 5 — The Cardinal Rules

| DO | AVOID |
|----|-------|
| Write toward a known ending | Starting without knowing your character's Lie |
| Anchor WHO/WHERE/WHEN in first lines of every scene | Filter words (saw, heard, felt, noticed) |
| Strong verbs, specific nouns | Adverbs masking weak verbs |
| Show emotion through action and detail | Announcing emotion: "She was angry" |
| One precise image | Three ornate synonyms for the same sensory beat |
| Character flaw drives both arc and plot | Obstacles from coincidence, not character |
| Honour genre contracts | Betraying HEA in romance; deus ex machina magic in fantasy |
| Read widely in your target genre before writing it | Writing a genre you haven't deeply consumed |
| Sexual tension through restraint and withholding | Explicit scenes before emotional groundwork |
| Prose serves story | Prose that calls attention to itself |
